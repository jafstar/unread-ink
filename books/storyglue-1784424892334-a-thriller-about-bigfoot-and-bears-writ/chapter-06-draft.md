The hum intensified, a low thrum that bypassed Nora's ears, settling in her bones. It pulled her deeper, a magnetic north in the dense fog. The tracks ahead were undeniable now, not just prints but disturbed earth, crushed ferns, a path carved with purpose. The creature was no longer hiding. It was laying a trail.

She moved with an almost primal instinct, rifle held ready, every nerve thrumming in sync with the mountain's bass pulse. The drizzle had softened the forest floor, muffling her steps, amplifying the silence between the creature's resonant calls. The whitebark groves blurred into a wall of gray, then thinned. She pushed through a curtain of dripping branches and emerged onto a narrow ledge overlooking a creek.

And there it was.

The second missing hiker. A young man, his pack neatly placed beside him like a pillow. His hands were folded across his chest. His eyes were closed, as if in peaceful sleep.

Nora stopped. The scene was a grotesque parody of rest. She had seen bodies before, mauled and torn, victims of nature's indifference. This was different. This was *arranged*.

She approached, her boots crunching softly on the wet gravel. The hum receded slightly, a patient withdrawal. Closer, she saw the details. The hiker's face was pale, serene. Too serene. The fabric of his shirt, pulled tight across his chest, revealed something beneath.

Nora knelt, gloved fingers trembling slightly as she unbuttoned his shirt. Her breath hitched. The man's chest cavity was a hollowed-out shell. Organs removed. Not torn, not ripped by teeth or claw. *Extracted*. Surgically precise. The edges of the openings were clean, almost cauterized.

And on his ribs, just beneath the collarbone, were claw marks. Four parallel lines, etched into the bone. Identical in spacing, in depth, to the marks she'd seen on the hemlock tree where the first hiker had been cached. *Too precise. Almost tool-like.* Her own words from that first scene echoed, chilling her to the core. This was the same hand. The same method.

She fumbled for her radio, her mind screaming for Danny, for backup, for any human voice. "Danny, come in. Danny, this is Nora. I've found—"

Static roared in her ear. A blinding burst of white noise, then silence. Utter, complete silence. The hum had vanished. The forest seemed to hold its breath.

Her signal was gone. The creature had cut her off. Isolated her.

The light began to fail, bleeding from gray to bruised purple. The air grew colder, heavy with unspoken menace. Nora documented the scene, moving with a desperate urgency, snapping photos, taking samples from the ground around the body, from the hollowed chest cavity. Every movement felt watched. Every shadow deepened into a lurking presence.

As darkness descended, plunging the basin into a deep, starless black, the fog thickened, shrouding everything in a suffocating gray. Nora knew she couldn't stay by the body. Not here. Not alone.

She packed her gear, senses straining into the oppressive dark. Her skin crawled. The hum was back, faint now, a barely perceptible tremor in the rock beneath her feet. It was closer.

Then she heard it. A snort. The rustle of heavy brush. A low growl, deep in its throat. Not the patient watchfulness of the black bear from yesterday. This was agitated. This was hunting.

A bear. Black bear. Heading straight for her.

Nora moved. No time for thought, only instinct. She scrambled, rifle still clutched, toward the sheer rock face that formed one side of the creek bed. Her boots found purchase on slick granite, fingers scrabbling for handholds. The bear growled again, closer, the sound vibrating through the rock.

She climbed, muscles burning, breath ragged, ignoring the scrapes and gashes. Up, up, until she found a small ledge, barely wide enough for her to press her back against the cold stone. Fifty feet above the creek bed. She could hear the bear below, snuffling, its heavy footsteps circling. It wasn't just passing through. It was looking for her.

Then, from the inky blackness at the base of the cliff, a form resolved.

Massive. Bipedal. It stood there, silhouetted against the slightly lighter gray of the creek bed, perfectly still. Taller than any man, broader than any bear. Its outline was blurred by the fog, but its presence was undeniable. No movement. No sound. Just a silent, unwavering watchfulness.

The Handler.

The bear below, still circling, let out a deep huff. It paused, nose twitching. Then, a low sound emanated from the Handler. Not a roar. Not a growl. A resonant pulse, like the hum, but layered with a clear, undeniable command. It rippled through the air, vibrating the very rock Nora clung to.

The bear stopped. It stood still for a moment, then turned. Without another sound, without a backward glance, it padded away, melting back into the thicket until the forest swallowed its presence whole.

Nora clung to the cliff face, muscles screaming, heart hammering a frantic rhythm. The Handler remained below, unmoving. It had controlled the bear. It had guided her to the body. It had cut her off.

It wasn't here to kill her.

A new, terrifying clarity washed over her. It wasn't hunting her. It was communicating. It was showing her something. It wanted her to understand.

And it wanted her to follow.

Nora looked down at the silent, colossal figure at the base of the cliff, then out into the impenetrable darkness of the basin. The true hunt was just beginning.
