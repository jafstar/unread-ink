Nora sat across from Deputy Director Marcus Leland in the ranger station's conference room, the fluorescent lights overhead casting harsh shadows across his weathered face. A stack of incident reports lay between them, each one stamped with red ink: PENDING REVIEW.

"You understand the situation," Leland said, his voice flat. "One hiker dead. Two survivors with no memory of the incident. And your report—" He tapped the topmost folder. "—reads like something out of a cryptid blog."

Nora kept her hands folded in her lap, willing herself to stillness. "I reported what I observed."

"A bipedal creature. Controlling bears. Thermal anomalies." Leland's eyebrow arched. "Nora, I've known you for three years. You're one of our best. But this—"

"Is the truth."

"The truth," Leland interrupted, "is that the Forest Service has classified this incident as a mass psychological event. Environmental stressors, dehydration, possible contamination from natural gas seepage in the basin. The survivors' memory loss supports that theory."

Nora's jaw tightened. "They didn't lose their memories from gas seepage."

"According to the medical evaluation, they show signs of acute dissociative amnesia. Trauma-induced. Consistent with exposure to extreme stress." Leland leaned forward. "What they don't show is any physical evidence of an attack by an unknown creature."

"Because it didn't attack them. It—"

"Enough." Leland's voice cut through the room like a blade. "Effective immediately, you're suspended pending the completion of our investigation. Two weeks, minimum. You'll surrender your badge and service weapon. Danny Moore will assume your patrol duties."

The words landed like stones. Nora sat very still, feeling the walls of the room press inward.

"This is a mistake," she said quietly.

"This is protocol." Leland stood, signaling the end of the meeting. "Go home, Nora. Rest. When the investigation concludes, we'll reassess."

That night, Nora sat in her darkened apartment, laptop open on the kitchen table. She had promised Danny she'd stay out of it. She hadn't promised to stop looking.

She pulled up the satellite imagery archives, scrolling through thermal data from the Thunder Creek Basin over the past six months. The software was clunky, outdated, but she knew how to coax information from it. She narrowed the parameters, focusing on the area where she'd encountered the Handler, where the spiral of stones had been laid.

At first, nothing stood out. Standard thermal signatures: cooler at night, warmer during the day, with minor fluctuations from weather patterns.

Then she saw it.

A warm spot, small but distinct, appearing in the same location every forty-eight hours. It would bloom, a gentle orange glow on the thermal overlay, persist for approximately six hours, then fade back to ambient temperature. The cycle repeated with mechanical precision.

Nora leaned closer, her pulse quickening. She cross-referenced the dates. The pattern had begun three months ago, coinciding with the first of the unusual bear sightings logged in the old ranger's journal.

She ran the numbers again, checked the calibration, verified the data source. No error. The heat pulse was real, and it was regular. Like something breathing warmth into the ground.

She sat back, the implications unfolding in her mind. This wasn't random. This was controlled. Engineered.

She needed proof. Real, tangible proof that couldn't be dismissed as environmental contamination or psychological stress.

She scrolled through her contacts, stopping on a name she hadn't called in years: Aris Colwin. They'd been in grad school together, both studying environmental systems, before their paths diverged—hers into field conservation, his into geophysics. He was unconventional, curious, and most importantly, willing to bend the rules for a good mystery.

She typed out a message, careful to keep it vague enough to avoid suspicion but compelling enough to hook his interest.

*Aris—Need a favor. Geothermal anomaly in the Cascades. Off the books. Interested?*

The reply came within minutes.

*Always. When?*

Nora allowed herself a small, grim smile. She typed back a time and coordinates, then closed the laptop.

Two days later, she met Aris at the trailhead.

Nora watched the road unspool under the glow of her headlights, the needle of the speedometer hovering at the limit. The conversation with Aris replayed in her mind, his fascination a much-needed spark after days of official dismissal and lingering doubt.

By the time she reached the trailhead, the sun was a mere suggestion on the horizon, the sky a deepening bruise. Aris was already there, unloading a portable ground-penetrating radar unit from his battered SUV. He waved one lanky arm, a wide grin on his face, enthusiasm radiating off him like static electricity.

"Ready for a little geophysical sleuthing?" he called as she approached. The air was crisp, laden with the scent of wet pine and the promise of rain.

"More than ready," Nora said, trying to match his energy, though shadows still clung to her thoughts. She hoisted her pack onto her shoulders, the familiar weight of her rifle across her back both comforting and ominous.

Aris handed her one end of the radar unit, a surprisingly lightweight contraption with a dull metallic sheen. "Let's hope we find something that isn't just an odd heat bloom. Though, given your urgency, I suspect there's more to this than meets the eye."

"Trust me, Aris, if this is what I think it is, tonight might redefine bizarre."

They fell into a steady rhythm, their boots squelching softly through the damp undergrowth. The hike in silence was a respite in itself, the forest wrapping them in its whispering embrace. Nora found solace in Aris's calm presence, his occasional mutterings about sediment layers and subsoil compositions a balm to her frayed nerves.

As they pushed deeper, the forest grew denser, shadows merging into a monochrome landscape that seemed to hold its breath. Nora's senses sharpened; her eyes traced every movement, every perceived shift in the undergrowth. The memory of her last trek through these woods lingered—eyes watching from the mist, the Handler's pulsing arm-lines an unnerving beacon.

The radar's soft whirr joined the symphony of nocturnal sounds as they neared the basin. Aris paused, consulting his handheld monitor and glancing around. "This is the spot, according to your coordinates."

Nora nodded, her gaze fixed on the spot a few meters ahead where the forest floor dipped slightly—a deceptively mundane locus for something so out of place.

Aris set up the radar, his long fingers flying over the controls with practiced ease. As data began streaming onto the small screen, he crouched, frowned, adjusted a few knobs. "There's definitely something unusual here. The subsurface is warmer than it should be, and there's this... rhythmic distortion."

Nora peered over his shoulder as an unfamiliar pattern emerged—a gentle, undulating wave that danced across the screen like a heartbeat felt through stone. "That's it. That's what I saw on the satellite imagery."

Aris whistled low. "It's like the earth herself is breathing. A heat pulse this regular, this deep... it's not just geological. I've never seen anything like this."

Nora's heart thudded in echo to the pulse on the screen. "It aligns with the forty-eight-hour cycle I observed. What could cause that?"

Aris shook his head, eyes locked on the monitor. "In theory? Some kind of geothermal anomaly. But this pattern suggests something more sophisticated. Like it's active, controlled."

Her mind raced, piecing together memories of the Handler's silent command over the bears, the precision with which it carved up its territory. "Could this be a sort of communication? A way to interact with something in the environment?"

A speculative light flickered in Aris's eyes. "It's possible. If this is some form of natural infrastructure—an engineered aspect of the terrain interacting with ambient conditions."

Nora considered this, the weight of revelation settling into her bones. "Could it be tied to the missing hikers? To what's happening with the bears?"

Aris paused, considering. "If the heat pulse influences them, draws them even. Sure. But how?"

"That's what I need to find out." Determination solidified within her. "I have to understand what this Handler is doing. And why."

Aris glanced up, catching her intense expression, the resolve burning behind her eyes. "Radio isn't going to work down here—the geology will eat the signal. If you plan on going further in, you'll need some way to verify this is real, to get others on board."

He hesitated, then added with uncharacteristic softness, "Be careful, Nora. This thing—whatever it is—you're too close for it to just let you go."

She met his gaze, appreciation softening her features. "Thank you, Aris. For coming. For believing."

He gave her a wry smile. "It's hard not to, with a mystery this compelling. I've got your back."

They dismantled the radar, the quiet resolve of their preparations underscoring the gravity of what lay ahead. As they began the trek back, Aris set his usual levity aside, matching the solemnity of the forest around them.

Returning to her car, Nora felt a renewed urgency, a fierce, unyielding drive to lift the veil on what simmered beneath the earth—a truth that no official report could sanitize, no cautious word could contain. She would find answers, or she would find understanding in their pursuit.

Tomorrow, she vowed silently, she would return to the forest. She had more to learn, more to uncover. She understood her role now. Not as a hunter. As a witness. And this time, she'd be ready for whatever the Handler had to show her.
