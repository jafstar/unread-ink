The stone beneath our feet shifted with deliberate intention. Not earthquake tremors—controlled movement, precise as clockwork.

"What's happening?" Tawa's hand found my shoulder, steadying himself.

The chamber floor rippled in concentric circles, emanating from the tablets at its center. The tubes pulsed faster, their light strobing in patterns that hurt to watch directly. I squeezed my eyes shut, but the rhythm penetrated anyway—felt rather than seen.

When I opened them again, the tablets had changed. The symbols no longer writhed randomly. They'd locked into fixed positions, arranged in deliberate rows that reminded me of something I'd seen before. Not language. Not exactly.

"They're waiting," I said. The words came from somewhere deeper than conscious thought. "They need something from us."

"Fragments." His voice cracked. "I was young. I didn't pay attention the way I should have."

"Try."

He closed his eyes, lips moving silently. Then, haltingly, he began to speak. The words were Hopi, but older than any dialect I'd heard him use before. Guttural, weighted with ceremony. His pronunciation stumbled, uncertain, but the chamber responded.

"Keep going," I urged.

He continued, voice gaining confidence as muscle memory overtook conscious recall. Words about emergence. About breath. About the covenant between stone and sky, earth and those who walked upon it.

The symbols completed their transformation. What had been random script now formed a clear sequence—glyphs that mirrored formations I'd mapped on the surface. I fumbled for my phone, hands shaking. The grinding was deafening now, punctuated by the sharp crack of stone fracturing. The sealed entrance wouldn't hold much longer.

"Document it," Tawa said. "Quickly."

Not gradually—all at once, as if a switch had been thrown. The silver tubes dimmed to faint luminescence. The chamber plunged into near-darkness, lit only by the dying glow from the tubes and the harsh white light of my phone screen.

The Handlers.
