**The Collapse Begins**

We rest in the greenhouse for what feels like hours but is probably only minutes, the warmth and the living light offering a reprieve we both desperately need. Tawa sits cross-legged near the carved wall, eyes closed, his breathing slow and meditative. I lean against a cluster of luminescent fungi, my leg elevated, letting the pain ebb to something manageable.

The canyon breathes around us. The tubes hum their steady harmony. For the first time since entering this place, I allow myself to simply exist within it, not as a researcher, not as my father's daughter, but as a witness.

Then the harmony breaks.

The tubes' pulse stutters, their light flickering erratically. A grinding sound echoes from somewhere far above—distant but growing closer, mechanical and deliberate.

Tawa's eyes snap open. "What—"

The explosion hits like a physical fist, and the greenhouse shudders violently. Dust cascades from the ceiling, illuminated by the frantic pulsing of the silver tubes. There's a low, grinding roar echoing from somewhere above, growing louder by the second.

"What was that?" Tawa shouts, springing to his feet.

The bioluminescent plants flicker erratically. Waves of light ripple across the cavern, then die section by section. The vibrant blues and greens of the fungi fade to a sickly yellow and blink out. The air that was once warm and sweet turns cold and thin. The tubes, once harmonious, now shriek discordantly.

"Charges," I choke out, pulling my phone from my pocket. My hands tremble, but I manage to open the holographic map from the central library. The ghost canyon projection shimmers, unstable but visible.

Explosive icons appear on the map, dotting the upper shafts that lead to the greenhouse. "They're trying to bring down the entire upper section," I say, voice raw. "Seal us in forever."

Tawa grips my arm, pulling me toward the carved wall where the narrative frieze is cracking, dust sifting from the ancient stone. "Where's the exit?"

My finger hovers over the map, landing on a thin, nearly invisible fissure near the ceiling. "There. An air shaft to the Tower of Ra."

"Then let's go." His eyes scan for a path, settling on a cluster of vines still glowing against the wall.

"Wait." I point to another symbol on the map, a red icon resting directly on the shaft. "It goes through a chamber... already rigged with their explosives."

Tawa freezes. "What does that mean for us?"

"It means we could be climbing into a bomb."

The greenhouse heaves again, a deeper tremor running through it. More plants wink out, shriveling away. The air is thick with the scent of ozone and crumbling stone.

"How much time?" Tawa demands.

I shake my head, watching fissures spread across the ceiling. "The collapse is a chain reaction. We have minutes, at best."

"Then we go now," he insists, pointing to thick, twisted vines hanging from the wall. "Use them like ropes."

I jump to catch the lowest vine, my leg screaming in agony, threatening to give way. Tawa boosts me up with surprising strength, and my fingers latch onto the bioluminescent surface. It's slick but firm, pulsing with fading warmth.

"Climb!" Tawa yells, grabbing his own vine.

I pull myself upward, hand over hand, using the vines like lifelines. My shoulders burn. My bad leg dangles uselessly, dead weight that threatens to drag me down. Below, the greenhouse is dying—fronds collapse, and luminescent flora flicker out. Everything is unraveling. The tubes writhe violently, their light spasming as if in pain.

Tawa is ahead, moving with deliberate strength, finding handholds I can't see in the failing light. "Almost there, Petra! Just a little more!"

I glance down. The floor is now a shadowy mass of dead lights. Dust and smoke swirl upwards, choking the air. Then I hear it—a sharp *CRACK* that echoes through the chamber, followed by a rumble that shakes the vines in my hands.

The first charge.

"Now!" Tawa screams.

I reach the fissure, a narrow split in the stone barely wide enough for my shoulders. Tawa hauls me in as the cavern behind us erupts. Heat sears my back—a blast of superheated air that singes my hair and turns the dying greenhouse into an inferno.

We crawl, blind and desperate. The passage is tight, scraping skin from my elbows and knees. My bad leg drags behind me, useless, catching on every irregularity in the stone. The roar of the collapse follows us, growing louder, closer, the sound of tons of rock giving way.

The passage turns upward at a sharp angle. I claw at the stone, pulling myself higher, ignoring the blood slicking my palms. Behind us, another explosion—closer this time, the shockwave compressing the air in the tunnel until my ears pop.

"Keep moving!" Tawa's voice, hoarse and strained.

The passage widens slightly. I can lift my head now, can see the silver tubes lining the walls ahead, their light steady and calm, guiding us upward. The air clears, the heat less oppressive. My lungs drag in desperate breaths.

We tumble into an upward-sloping tunnel, smooth-walled and wider, the tubes humming with renewed harmony. I collapse against the wall, chest heaving, every muscle trembling. My leg gives way, a sharp spike of white-hot pain driving through my knee. I cannot stand. I drag myself forward with my arms, leaving a smear of blood on the stone floor. The fall did it—the wrong twist, the weight of my body landing wrong. Something has torn or shifted, and the leg will not bear me again soon.

Tawa crouches beside me, eyes scanning my face. "Can you walk?"

I shake my head. "Not on this."

Behind us, a terrible roar as the greenhouse collapses fully, the sound of the earth consuming itself. Dust billows through the passage we just escaped, a gray wave that stops just short of where we lie gasping.

For just a moment, before the dust obscures everything, I see it through the chaos—a vast cavern revealed by the violent upheaval of the collapse. The falling rock has torn away a hidden wall, exposing a space so immense the tubes lining its perimeter look like threads.

And suspended in the center, bathed in shimmering golden light, is a single colossal object.

It pulses.

*Thump... thump... thump.*

Not mechanical. Organic. Alive. A sphere easily fifty feet across, its surface covered in intricate patterns that shift and flow like living tissue. The silver tubes converge on it from every direction, feeding it, sustaining it, carrying its rhythm out into the canyon's vast network.

The serpent's heart.

Then, like a closing curtain, rock cascades down, sealing the breach. The vision vanishes behind tons of stone. It is gone.

But in that moment, something passes through me—a vibration, a resonance that is not sound but pressure and warmth together, a pulse that finds my own heartbeat and syncs with it. My chest fills with a sensation I cannot name: grief and belonging, vast and wordless, as if the heart has recognized me, has known me, and is now saying goodbye. It fades as quickly as it came, leaving me trembling on the cold stone floor.

Tawa and I lie on the tunnel floor, staring at the wall of debris where the greenhouse once lived and the heart briefly revealed itself. We're alive, suspended in a narrow slot of safety while the earth reshapes itself around us.

"Did you see it?" Tawa whispers.

"Yes."

"My grandmother was right. The breath isn't kept by technology. It's kept by something that still lives."

I nod, too exhausted for words. Somewhere far beneath us, sealed behind immeasurable stone, the serpent's heart continues its ancient rhythm, breathing its slow pulse into the canyon's veins as it has for millennia, as it will for millennia more.

The tubes around us settle into harmony, their light steady and sure, guiding us upward toward the surface. Toward the world that will never know what lies beneath their feet.

Tawa helps me to my feet, one arm around my waist, taking the weight of my injured leg across his shoulders. I lean into him, each step a negotiation with pain. The hand I scraped raw on the climb leaves a red print on his arm. He does not flinch.

We rise on shaking legs and begin the final climb.
