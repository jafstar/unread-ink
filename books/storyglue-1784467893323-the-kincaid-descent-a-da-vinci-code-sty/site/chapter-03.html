Chapter 3

The Confluence

The stone groaned around us, a deep, resonant sigh. The aftershocks of the collapse still vibrated through the rock, a persistent shiver that rattled my bones. Dust motes danced in the anemic glow of my phone, swirling like tiny ghosts in the suddenly quiet tunnel. I pressed my palm flat against the wall, feeling the tremors subside, and forced air into my burning lungs.

"Gone," Tawa whispered, his voice hoarse. "Completely gone."

I nodded, gripping my phone tighter. The silence was heavy, oppressive. The Handlers were stopped. For now. But at what cost? The entire chamber behind us had caved. Whatever ancient technology, whatever truth, we'd uncovered in that space was buried beneath tons of rock. My throat ached.

"We keep moving," I said, the words feeling brittle. My leg throbbed, a dull ache that spread from hip to ankle.

We walked deeper, the passage sloping relentlessly downward. The silver tubes here were different—thinner, duller, their light a mere suggestion of warmth. They seemed to wind us through the bedrock itself, not a carved tunnel but a natural fissure that had been somehow coaxed. It felt ancient, the stone worn smooth by eons of passage, or something more.

Minutes stretched into an eternity. My lungs burned with the thin air, my vision blurring with fatigue. The darkness was absolute beyond my phone's beam, a hungry void.

Then, a sound. Not the grinding of rock, or the whisper of unseen mechanisms. Water. A low, constant murmur, growing steadily louder with each step.

The passage opened abruptly.

We stumbled into a cavern so vast my phone beam was swallowed before reaching any wall. But a faint, otherworldly luminescence filled the space, a soft, diffuse glow emanating from below.

A river.

It was wide, obsidian black, flowing with an oily smoothness. And it pulsed—not with light, not exactly, but with the steady, rhythmic shimmer of a billion tiny bioluminescent organisms, like scattered starlight captured and submerged. The silver tubes continued here, lining the cavern walls like colossal, twisting roots, their light flickering in uneven patterns, each length pulsing at its own rhythm, as if the harmony that bound them had fractured.

"A river," Tawa breathed, awe in his voice. "The true rivers. My grandmother spoke of them."

I didn't speak. The sheer scale of it, the living shimmer, stole my breath. This wasn't just a subterranean waterway; it felt like the canyon's circulatory system. And those tubes, pulsing so erratically, were its frayed nerves.

We descended to the riverbank, the air suddenly heavy with the scent of ozone and wet stone. A fine mist hung above the water, catching the faint light. We began to follow the current, our boots crunching on ancient silt and gravel.

Carved pillars rose from the river's edge, colossal columns of dark stone, stretching up into the gloom until they vanished. Their surfaces were covered in intricate carvings, spirals and angular geometries. I shone my light on them, the beam catching the deep grooves.

"Kincaid," I murmured, tracing a hand over a familiar symbol—the coiled serpent. "His journal mentioned these. 'Watchers of the deep current.'"

Tawa stepped closer to a different pillar, his fingers brushing a series of interconnected squares. "No, this is older. Hopi. The emergence pathway. My people's journey from the underworlds, etched into the stone. The sipapu, the place of emergence." His voice was hushed, reverent. "These aren't just markers. They're a story."

We moved slowly, the river murmuring beside us, its bioluminescence ebbing and flowing in soft waves. The air grew warmer, humid. The discordant pulsing of the tubes grated on my nerves, a constant, low thrum that set my teeth on edge. They felt wrong—not frightened, but broken, like a signal gone to static.

The river widened ahead, bifurcating around a central island of dark rock. My breath caught.

On the island, silhouetted against the glowing water, sat a figure. Cross-legged. Still. Human-shaped.

My heart hammered against my ribs.

We waded through a shallow channel, the water cold against my shins, the bioluminescent organisms swirling around my legs like living dust. My phone beam, still weak, fought against the ambient glow as we approached.

It was a skeleton. Weathered bone, pristine white, sitting upright, facing upstream, as if meditating. Clutched in its bony hands was a Hopi medicine bundle, a small, tightly wrapped package of leather and feathers. And beneath that, pinned to what remained of a tattered shirt, a small, silver badge.

My vision tunneled.

I leaned closer, my breath hitching. USGS. United States Geological Survey. And the name beneath it. Clear as day, untouched by the passage of decades, as if it had been pinned there yesterday.

*Robert Keane. 1987.*

My father.

The air sucked from my lungs. My father. Not a madman. Not lost. Here. Preserved. A ghost in the current.

A wave of grief, cold and sharp, washed over me. Twenty years. Twenty years I'd carried his absence like a stone in my chest, the unanswered questions, the whispered doubts about his sanity. And here he sat, cross-legged on an island in an impossible river, holding a medicine bundle like a prayer.

"Petra." Tawa's voice was soft, careful.

I couldn't answer. My hand reached out, hovering over the badge. The metal gleamed in the bioluminescent glow, impossibly pristine. No rust. No corrosion. As if time itself had chosen to preserve this one artifact, this one proof.

My fingers brushed the edge of the badge.

The river exploded.

Not water—light. The bioluminescent organisms erupted from the surface in a massive swarm, a billion pinpoints of living radiance that surrounded us in a blinding sphere. I threw my arm up, squinting against the intensity, but the light penetrated my closed eyelids, my raised hand, my skull.

And then I saw him.

My father. Alive. Younger than I remembered but older than the photograph I carried. He sat on this same island, journal open on his lap, writing frantically. The medicine bundle lay beside him, unwrapped. His face was drawn, exhausted, but his eyes were clear. Focused.

He looked up suddenly, pen freezing mid-stroke. His expression shifted—not fear, exactly. Recognition. As if he'd been expecting this moment.

A shadow moved at the edge of the vision. Human-shaped but wrong somehow, the proportions distorted by the swirling light. It approached slowly, deliberately.

My father didn't run. He closed the journal with careful precision, placed the pen beside it. He picked up the medicine bundle and began to wrap it, his movements ritualistic, purposeful. His lips moved, forming words I couldn't hear.

The shadow reached him.

The vision cracked like struck glass.

The swarm dispersed as suddenly as it had risen, the organisms settling back into the black water with barely a ripple. I gasped, staggering, my hand still touching the badge. Tawa caught my shoulder, steadying me.

"What did you see?" His voice was urgent.

"My father. Writing. And something came for him. Someone."

"The Handlers?"

"I don't know." My chest heaved. "He wasn't afraid. He was... prepared."

I looked down at the skeleton, at the medicine bundle still clutched in its hands. My father had wrapped it after that shadow arrived. Whatever he'd seen, whatever he'd learned, he'd sealed it into that bundle before he died.

Or before he chose to stay.

On the skeleton's lap, partially hidden beneath the medicine bundle, lay a single page of paper. The fibers were brittle but intact, preserved by the dry air or something else. I lifted the bundle with infinite care and slid the page free.

It was a sketch. The cavern. The river. The pillars. All rendered in my father's precise hand, every line deliberate. And beneath it, a single word in his distinctive script:

*Listen.*

The word seemed to pulse on the page. And then, from somewhere deep beneath the river, I felt it—a rhythm. Not heard. Felt. *Thump-thump-thump.* Slow, massive, ancient. A heartbeat that wasn't mine, wasn't human, but was undeniably alive.

The canyon's pulse.

I looked at the badge still gleaming on my father's chest, at the medicine bundle in my trembling hands, at the sketch that commanded me to listen.

He hadn't been killed. He hadn't been silenced.

He had listened. And whatever he'd heard had been worth staying for.

I folded the sketch carefully and tucked it into my pocket beside Kincaid's journal. The river murmured around us, the bioluminescent organisms drifting in lazy spirals. The tubes on the cavern walls continued their discordant pulse, broken but persistent.

"What did he mean?" Tawa asked. "Listen to what?"

I placed my palm flat against the island's stone. The heartbeat thrummed through the rock, through my bones, through everything.

"To this," I said. "To whatever's been breathing down here all along."
