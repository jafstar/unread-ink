The stone groaned around us, a deep, resonant sigh. The aftershocks of the collapse still vibrated through the rock, a persistent shiver that rattled my bones. Dust motes danced in the anemic glow of my phone, swirling like tiny ghosts in the suddenly quiet tunnel. We stood, breathless, ears straining for any sound from the passage we'd just fled. Nothing. Just the fading hum of the tubes, now barely visible against the rough-hewn walls.

"Gone," Tawa whispered, his voice hoarse. "Completely gone."

I nodded, gripping my phone tighter. The silence was heavy, oppressive. The Handlers were stopped. For now. But at what cost? The entire chamber behind us had caved. Whatever ancient technology, whatever truth, we'd uncovered in that space was buried beneath tons of rock. My throat ached.

"We keep moving," I said, the words feeling brittle. My leg throbbed, a dull ache that spread from hip to ankle.

We walked deeper, the passage sloping relentlessly downward. The silver tubes here were different—thinner, duller, their light a mere suggestion of warmth. They seemed to be winding us through the bedrock itself, not a carved tunnel but a natural fissure that had been somehow… coaxed. It felt ancient, the stone worn smooth by eons of passage, or something more.

Minutes stretched into an eternity. My lungs burned with the thin air, my vision blurring with fatigue. The darkness was absolute beyond my phone's beam, a hungry void.

Then, a sound. Not the grinding of rock, or the whisper of unseen mechanisms. Water. A low, constant murmur, growing steadily louder with each step.

The passage opened abruptly.

We stumbled into a cavern of impossible scale. My phone beam was swallowed by the vastness, rendering it useless. But a faint, otherworldly luminescence filled the space, a soft, diffuse glow emanating from below.

A river.

It was wide, obsidian black, flowing with an oily smoothness. And it pulsed. Not with light, not exactly, but with the steady, rhythmic shimmer of a billion tiny bioluminescent organisms. Like scattered starlight captured and submerged. The silver tubes continued here, lining the cavern walls like colossal, twisting roots, their dull light flickering in sync with the river's slow undulation. But their pulse was discordant now, a jumble of rhythms, as if agitated.

"A river," Tawa breathed, awe in his voice. "The true rivers. My grandmother spoke of them."

I didn't speak. The sheer impossible beauty, the scale of it, stole my breath. This wasn't just a subterranean waterway; it felt like the canyon's living circulatory system. And those tubes, pulsing so erratically, were its nerves.

We descended to the riverbank, the air suddenly heavy with the scent of ozone and wet stone. A fine mist hung above the water, catching the faint light. We began to follow the current, our boots crunching on ancient silt and gravel.

Carved pillars rose from the river's edge, colossal columns of dark stone, stretching up into the gloom until they vanished. Their surfaces were covered in intricate carvings, spirals and angular geometries. I shone my light on them, the beam catching the deep grooves.

"Kincaid," I murmured, tracing a hand over a familiar symbol—the coiled serpent. "His journal mentioned these. 'Watchers of the deep current.'"

Tawa stepped closer to a different pillar, his fingers brushing a series of interconnected squares. "No, this is older. Hopi. The emergence pathway. My people's journey from the underworlds, etched into the stone. The sipapu, the place of emergence." His voice was hushed, reverent. "These aren't just markers. They're a story."

We moved slowly, the river murmuring beside us, its bioluminescence ebbing and flowing in soft waves. The air grew warmer, humid. The discordant pulsing of the tubes grated on my nerves, a constant, low thrum of distress. They felt alive, and they felt... frightened.

The river widened ahead, bifurcating around a central island of dark rock. My breath caught.

On the island, silhouetted against the glowing water, sat a figure. Cross-legged. Still. Human-shaped.

My heart hammered against my ribs.

We waded through a shallow channel, the water cold against my shins, the bioluminescent organisms swirling around my legs like living dust. My phone beam, still weak, fought against the ambient glow as we approached.

It was a skeleton. Weathered bone, pristine white, resting in an impossible state of preservation. It sat upright, facing upstream, as if meditating. Clutched in its bony hands was a Hopi medicine bundle, a small, tightly wrapped package of leather and feathers. And beneath that, pinned to what remained of a tattered shirt, a small, silver badge.

My vision tunneled.

I leaned closer, my breath hitching. USGS. United States Geological Survey. And the name beneath it. Clear as day, untouched by the passage of decades, as if it had been pinned there yesterday.

*Robert Keane. 1987.*

My father.

I froze, the air sucked from my lungs. My father. Not a madman. Not lost. Here. Preserved. A ghost in the current.

A wave of grief, cold and sharp, washed over me, followed by a surge of desperate hope. He wasn't gone. He was *here*. This wasn't madness. This was discovery. His discovery.

My hand reached out, trembling, for the badge. I had to touch it. To feel the proof. To know it was real.

The instant my fingertips brushed the cold metal, the river erupted.

The bioluminescence flared, impossibly bright, a blinding white light that shot upward from the black water. A low, throbbing *thrum* echoed through the cavern, not from the tubes, but from the river itself.

Then, from the water, a Swarm. Millions of tiny, glowing organisms rose as one, like a liquid aurora, swirling into a dense, incandescent cloud that enveloped us. We were surrounded, immersed in pure light. My eyes burned, my ears rang with the sudden, overwhelming hum of their collective energy.

Through the dazzling chaos, a vision bloomed. Not just light, but imagery. My father. Younger, alive. He sat on this very island, his back to me, writing furiously in a small, leather-bound journal. His face was etched with concentration, a flicker of excitement in his eyes. He paused, ink poised over the page, then slowly, deliberately, he looked up.

His eyes widened. Not with excitement now, but with absolute terror. His mouth opened in a silent scream.

Behind him, a shadow. Vast. Formless. Rising from the black water like an emerging god.

The vision cracked, splintered, then shattered.

The Swarm dispersed, falling back into the river like luminous rain. The cavern plunged into relative darkness again, lit only by the normal, undulating glow of the water. Tawa and I stood gasping, drenched in sweat, our hearts pounding. My hand was still outstretched, inches from my father's skeletal chest.

I stared at the skeleton. Untouched. The badge gleamed.

On his lap, beneath the medicine bundle, a single page of paper had been neatly tucked. Untouched by decay. I picked it up, my fingers shaking.

It was a sketch of the cavern, the river, the pillars. And beneath it, in my father's distinctive, elegant hand, a single word.

*Listen.*

The word seemed to vibrate off the page. And then, from the depths of the black river, a sound began to rise. Not the hum of the Swarm. Not the thrum of the tubes. Something deeper. Something vast. A rhythmic *thump-thump-thump*, felt in the soles of my feet, in the deepest part of my skull. A heartbeat. But one so immense, so ancient, it could only belong to the canyon itself.
