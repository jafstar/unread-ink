# Pass 2 report — The Breath of the Serpent (chapters 1-3)

The manuscript exhibits several distinct and recurring prose patterns that function as stylistic signatures but risk becoming noticeable tics at book length. Most pervasive is the 'Not X' negation-as-reveal construction, appearing 15+ times across three chapters—a technique that works powerfully in isolation but becomes formulaic when repeated so frequently. Closely related is the heavy reliance on em-dash interruptions to refine or correct a description mid-sentence (25+ instances), creating a halting, self-correcting rhythm. The prose also shows a strong preference for single-word or very short fragment sentences as dramatic punctuation (20+ times), and a recurring insistence on the literal physicality of sensations through explicit denial of metaphor ('Not a metaphor,' 'Not abstract,' 'The real weight'). These patterns create a distinctive voice—visceral, precise, resistant to abstraction—but their frequency makes them predictable. A reader who notices the 'Not X' pattern in Chapter 1 will see it everywhere afterward, potentially breaking immersion. The triadic rhythm in descriptive passages is less obtrusive but still noticeable. Overall, the prose would benefit from varying these constructions more deliberately, reserving the 'Not X' negation for truly crucial moments rather than using it as a default intensifier.
