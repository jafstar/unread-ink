**The Hunters**

The drone's buzz fills the library chamber, echoing off the concentric rings of tablets. I spin, searching the projection still rotating above the central tablet, looking for any exit marked in the ghost canyon's architecture.

"There." Tawa points to a cluster of symbols at the library's far edge. "Ventilation. It has to be."

The drone descends into view at the shaft opening, its camera sweeping across the chamber. Behind it, I hear voices—controlled, professional. The Handlers coordinating their descent.

We run.

The tablet rings form a maze, forcing us to weave between spirals. My bad leg screams with every step, threatening to buckle. Behind us, boots hit stone. Four figures drop from the shaft in rapid succession, dressed in black tactical gear, faces obscured by respirators. One carries a cutting torch, its pilot light casting dancing shadows. The others hold weapons—compact, suppressed rifles that look designed for enclosed spaces.

"Split up," one barks, voice muffled by the mask. "Two on each side. Box them in."

Tawa veers left. I follow, ducking behind a cluster of taller tablets arranged like stone pillars. The symbols on their surfaces flicker wildly as we pass, responding to movement or fear or something else I don't understand.

A burst of suppressed gunfire cracks the air. Stone chips explode from the tablet beside my head. I drop, crawling now, using the low-hanging tubes as cover. They pulse with alarmed intensity, their light strobing in patterns that make my vision swim.

Tawa is ahead, moving with practiced silence despite his size. He knows how to use shadow, how to blend with stone. I try to follow his lead, controlling my breathing, ignoring the pain radiating from my hip.

Another burst. Closer. The Handlers are converging, using the tablet rings to triangulate our position.

We reach the outer edge of the library where the tablets give way to natural stone formations—spiraled pillars carved by ancient water, their surfaces slick with mineral deposits. Tawa presses against one, gesturing for silence.

Footsteps approach. Methodical. Two sets, moving in coordinated sweeps.

I grip the nearest tablet—a small one, about the size of a dinner plate. The symbols on its surface writhe beneath my fingers, growing brighter, hotter. Whatever these tablets are, they respond to stress.

A Handler rounds the pillar, rifle raised. His mask turns toward me, featureless and terrible.

I throw the tablet.

It catches him square in the chest. For a heartbeat nothing happens. Then the tablet explodes in a burst of golden light so intense I have to shield my eyes. The Handler screams—a raw, animal sound—as his rifle disintegrates in his hands, the metal vaporizing in the blast. The light sears across his face where the mask has cracked, and he staggers backward, clutching his eyes.

"Contact! West side! Subject is armed with—"

His radio cuts off as he collapses, convulsing.

"Fall back!" Another voice, sharp with command. "Regroup at the center. They've got the artifacts."

The remaining three Handlers retreat toward the central tablet, moving with renewed caution. We use the chaos to run, stumbling through the maze of pillars toward the chamber's far wall.

There—a gap in the stone, narrow and low. The silver tubes converge around it, their light pointing the way like arrows.

"Ventilation shaft," Tawa confirms, dropping to his knees. "It's tight."

"We don't have a choice."

He goes first, squirming into the opening. I follow, forcing my body into the confined space. The shaft is barely wide enough for my shoulders. I have to exhale completely to make progress, using my elbows to drag myself forward inch by inch.

Behind us, voices echo in the library. The Handlers regrouping, planning their next move.

The shaft angles upward, then levels out. My arms shake with exhaustion. My bad leg drags uselessly behind me, dead weight. But I keep moving, following the glow of the tubes lining the passage.

After what feels like hours but is probably minutes, the shaft opens.

We spill out onto a narrow ledge carved into the canyon wall. My first breath of open air in what feels like days hits my lungs like ice water. The ledge is maybe three feet wide, littered with loose rock and ancient rubble. To our right, the wall rises sheer and unclimbable. To our left, a chasm drops into darkness so complete my phone's light can't find the bottom.

And directly across the chasm, maybe fifteen feet away, another ledge. Wider. Leading to a passage that glows with the faint silver light of more tubes.

"No," I breathe. "No, I can't."

"We have to." Tawa is already measuring the distance, calculating. "I'll go first. Test the landing."

"Tawa—"

He backs up three steps, then runs. His form is perfect—controlled, committed. He launches himself into the void, arms windmilling for balance.

He lands hard, stumbles, catches himself. Turns back to me.

"Your turn!" he shouts.

Behind me, grinding metal. The Handlers cutting through the ventilation shaft with the torch. They'll be here in seconds.

I look at the chasm. Fifteen feet. On a good day, with a running start and two working legs, maybe I could make it. Today is not a good day.

"Petra!" Tawa extends his hand across the gap, as if that could somehow bridge the distance. "You can do this!"

The shaft behind me glows orange with torch-light. I hear breathing—controlled, professional. A Handler emerging.

I back up as far as the ledge allows. Three steps. That's all I have.

The Handler's head appears from the shaft opening. Black mask. Blank lenses. A rifle barrel following close behind.

I run.

Three steps, then my bad leg plants, and I push off with everything I have. The canyon opens beneath me, a merciless void. Air screams past my ears. I stretch my arms toward Tawa, toward the ledge, toward anything.

Time dilates. I see every detail—the grain of the stone, the silver veins of tubes in the rock, Tawa's face contorted with hope and fear. I see my father's skeleton sitting eternal vigil by the underground river. I see my mother's photograph, creased and faded.

I see that I'm going to fall short.

My chest slams into the far ledge, knocking the air from my lungs. My fingers scrabble against stone, nails breaking, finding no purchase. I'm slipping, sliding backward into the abyss.

Then Tawa's hand clamps around my wrist. Iron-strong. Unbreakable.

"Got you!" he grunts, hauling me up with adrenaline-fueled strength.

I scramble onto the ledge, gasping, my entire body shaking. For a moment I can't move, can't think, can only lie on the stone and feel the solid earth beneath me.

On the far side of the chasm, the Handler steps fully onto the opposite ledge, rifle raised. Two more emerge behind him, spreading out.

The lead Handler aims.

And I hear it—a sound I've been hearing all along, but never truly understood. The rhythmic thrum of the tubes, pulsing through the canyon's veins. In the library, it was a heartbeat. But here, pressed against the stone, I feel its full meaning: resonance. The entire canyon is a single structure, a lattice of passages and chambers, every surface connected by the silver threads. The ledge I'm lying on—I can feel it vibrating at the same frequency as the tubes, as the tablets, as the central chamber. A standing wave, perfectly balanced.

And the Handler's ledge—it's vibrating at a different frequency. I felt the difference when I landed, a dissonant shudder through my palms.

The Handler's ledge is unstable because it's *resonating wrong*.

The lead Handler's finger tightens on the trigger.

"Now," I whisper, not to Tawa, not to the canyon—but to the tubes, as if they can hear.

The canyon answers.

A deep tremor rolls through the stone, starting where my body touches the ledge and spreading outward like a ripple. The chasm groans. Cracks race across the opposite ledge, spiderwebbing beneath the Handlers' feet.

The lead Handler's eyes widen behind his mask. He lowers the rifle, stepping backward. But there's nowhere to go. The ledge crumbles beneath him like sand, and he falls—a truncated scream swallowed by the abyss.

The other two Handlers scramble back toward the ventilation shaft as more of the ledge collapses, the canyon reshaping itself, sealing the passage.

Tawa stares at me, eyes wide. "What did you do?"

I look down at my hands, still pressed to the stone. I can still feel the frequency I transmitted—a note I didn't know I could sing, through a medium I didn't know I could command. The resonance is fading now, the canyon settling back into its rhythm.

"I don't know," I say. "But the library wasn't just a library. The tablets don't just record. They tune. Everything in this canyon is tuned to a single frequency—and the Handlers brought their own."

Tawa's expression shifts from shock to grim understanding. "They were dissonant."

"Yes."

He pulls me to my feet. We stumble into the passage behind us, not stopping until the sounds of the collapse fade into silence.

The passage slopes upward. The tubes guide us, their light steady now, calm. After everything, after the chaos and terror, they pulse with the same tidal rhythm that woke me in the library.

*Thump... thump... thump.*

The canyon's heartbeat, unchanged.

We climb in silence, too exhausted for words. My leg is beyond pain now, numb and distant. I count steps instead of thinking. One. Two. Ten. Twenty.

Light appears ahead. Not tube-glow. Sunlight.

We emerge onto a narrow trail carved into the canyon's upper wall. The sun is low, painting the layered stone in vivid reds and oranges. The vast expanse of the Grand Canyon spreads before us, beautiful and indifferent.

Behind us, deep in the earth, the ghost canyon breathes. The library holds its knowledge. My father keeps his vigil. The tablets record everything, updating in real-time, compensating, maintaining equilibrium.

And the Handlers—those who survived—will seal the passages, scrub the evidence, and deny everything.

Tawa and I stand on the ledge, watching the sun sink toward the rim. Neither of us speaks. There's nothing to say that the canyon hasn't already said for us.

Some truths can only be witnessed, not exposed. Some places exist to be remembered, not discovered.

The breath is not theirs to keep.

We begin the long climb toward the rim, toward the world that will never believe what we've seen. The canyon watches us go, patient as stone, old as time.

And somewhere far below, the serpent breathes.
