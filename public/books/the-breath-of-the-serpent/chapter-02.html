Chapter 2

The Serpent's Gate

The stone beneath our feet shifted with deliberate intention. Not earthquake tremors—controlled movement, precise as clockwork.

"What's happening?" Tawa's hand found my shoulder, steadying himself.

The chamber floor rippled in concentric circles, emanating from the tablets at their center. The tubes pulsed faster, their light strobing in patterns that hurt to watch directly. I squeezed my eyes shut, but the rhythm penetrated anyway—felt rather than seen.

When I opened them again, the tablets had changed. The symbols no longer writhed randomly. They'd locked into fixed positions, arranged in deliberate rows that reminded me of something I'd seen before. Not language. Not exactly.

The grinding outside intensified. Metal on stone, mechanical and ruthless. The Handlers cutting through the sealed entrance. We had minutes, maybe less.

"They're waiting," I said, the words tumbling out as the pressure of the moment cracked something open in my thinking. Because what else could it be? The tablets had responded to our arrival, to our touch, to every action we'd taken. They were watching. Testing. "They need something from us."

Tawa stared at the symbols. "My grandmother told me stories. About the First People, the ones who emerged from the sipapu. She said there were words—old words—that opened the way between worlds."

"Can you remember them?"

"Fragments." His voice cracked. "I was young. I didn't pay attention the way I should have."

The grinding grew louder. A high-pitched whine joined it—some kind of cutting tool. I imagined the seal thinning, the stone weakening, the Handlers' masked faces pressing close to the widening gap.

"Try," I said. "We don't have time for perfect."

He closed his eyes, lips moving silently. Then, haltingly, he began to speak. The words were Hopi, but older than any dialect I'd heard him use before. Guttural, weighted with ceremony. His pronunciation stumbled, uncertain, but the chamber responded.

The symbols on the tablets shifted.

I watched them rearrange—not smoothly, but in jerking movements, as though searching for something. One glyph would try a position, then recoil, shifting elsewhere. The pattern looked random, almost desperate. For a terrible second I thought Tawa had it wrong, that his imperfect memory would close the door instead of opening it.

"Keep going," I urged.

He continued, voice gaining confidence as muscle memory overtook conscious recall. Words about emergence. About breath. About the covenant between stone and sky, earth and those who walked upon it.

The symbols locked.

Not into a map. Not at first. Instead, the three tablets aligned to form something stranger—a configuration that didn't match any landmark I recognized. The glyphs showed Tower of Ra, yes, and Shiva Temple, the Temple of Isis. But they were positioned impossibly, their spatial relationships warped. Tower of Ra hung above the Temple of Isis. Shiva Temple occupied a point that didn't exist in the canyon's geography.

"It's wrong," I said. "That's not how they're laid out on the surface."

Tawa shook his head, still reciting. His voice had taken on a rhythmic quality, almost hypnotic.

I forced myself to look past the familiar silhouettes, to see the configuration as structure rather than representation. The landmarks weren't meant to be mapped spatially. They were being used as reference points for something else—a path through the underground system, perhaps, or a sequence that only made sense when read as directions rather than locations. The angular geometry between Tower of Ra and the Temple of Isis matched something I'd seen in petroglyphs from the Ancestral Puebloans. The same sacred geometry, encoded in stone.

The grinding outside intensified, punctuated by the sharp crack of stone fracturing. A cone of harsh LED light pierced through a growing crack in the entrance seal. Too close. The Handlers had breached the outer layer.

"Document it," Tawa said, his prayer faltering. "Quickly."

I fumbled for my phone, the sequence burning before me. Not just a path—a key. A configuration that connected the surface landmarks to something beneath. I raised the phone, framing all three tablets in the screen. The symbols burned almost too bright to capture, but I fired off shot after shot, the shutter clicking in rapid succession.

The instant I lowered the phone, the tablets went dark.

Not gradually—all at once, as if a switch had been thrown. The silver tubes dimmed to faint luminescence. The chamber plunged into near-darkness, lit only by the dying glow from the tubes, the harsh white light of my phone screen, and the LED beam stabbing through the cracked entrance.

"Did we break it?" My voice sounded small.

"No." Tawa pointed past me. "We opened it."

I spun around. The sealed wall behind us was grinding open. Not the same entrance we'd come through. A different passage, lower, narrower, descending at a sharp angle into darkness that seemed absolute.

The grinding from the other side intensified. A sharp metallic squeal, then a voice—clipped, professional, coordinating.

"Breach in thirty seconds."

The Handlers.

"Move!" I shoved Tawa toward the newly opened passage.

We scrambled toward the descending tunnel as the chamber behind us erupted in chaos. The crack in the entrance widened, admitting more light, more voices. Then came the pneumatic hiss of bolt cutters on reinforced stone.

We plunged into the new passage just as the first Handler's silhouette appeared in the breached entrance. I didn't look back to see their face. I focused on the darkness ahead, on Tawa's shape moving with desperate speed down the sloping tunnel.

Behind us, the chamber screamed.

Not metal on stone. Not equipment noise. The chamber itself emitted a grinding roar that shook the passage walls, a sound that seemed to come from the rock's deepest grain. The walls shuddered. Dust rained from above.

"Faster!" Tawa's shout barely registered.

I risked a glance back and saw the chamber ceiling beginning to collapse, tons of rock calving away in massive sheets. The tubes shattered, spraying luminescent fluid that hissed and steamed where it struck the floor.

A Handler's scream cut short.

The collapse accelerated, pursuing us down the passage. I felt the pressure wave against my back, hot air compressed by falling stone. My bad leg buckled but Tawa grabbed my arm, hauling me forward.

The passage twisted sharply. We rounded the bend as the roar reached its crescendo behind us, then cut off with shocking abruptness. The sudden silence was almost worse than the noise.

I stopped, gasping, leaning against the wall. Tawa bent double, hands on knees, dragging in ragged breaths.

"Are they..." I couldn't finish.

"I don't know." His voice was flat. "The chamber's gone. Whatever was behind us is sealed now."

I pulled out my phone, checking the photos with trembling hands. Still there. Blurry but legible—the glyph sequence, the impossible configuration, the key to whatever lay deeper.

The tubes in this new passage were darker, their pulse barely perceptible. But they were there, lining the walls, leading down into depths the surface world had never seen.

"We keep going," I said.

Tawa straightened, meeting my eyes. In the phone's dim glow, his face was unreadable.

"We have no choice now," he said. "The way back is gone."

Together, we turned toward the descending darkness, following the serpent's path into its heart.
