Chapter 5

The Handler

The rain had left behind a damp chill that seeped through Nora's clothes as she moved through the darkness toward Holman Peak. Thunder rolled across the mountain, a distant echo of what lay ahead, as she pressed deeper into the wild heart of the North Cascades. Her hands were steady, but her mind raced, replaying the journal's symbols and the whitebark resin under the microscope. She knew the mountains, the animals, the natural order of life and death in the woods. What she had found in that resin—cells that should not exist, structures that suggested organization, intent—was enough to make her question everything she thought she understood.

The storm broke over her in sheets, but she pushed forward through the night, following the drainage paths marked in the old ranger's journal. By dawn, the rain had softened to a persistent drizzle, fog clinging to the treetops in drifting wisps. Her radio crackled to life, a harsh burst of static before Danny's voice came through, strained and distant. "Nora, report in."

She paused, hand on the radio, weighing her words. The urge to tell him everything vied with a need to protect this secret until she understood it fully. "I'm here. Made it to the high country."

"Have you found any signs of the hikers?"

"Nothing concrete yet. The terrain's rough. I'm following some tracks, but the rain's washing things out."

A pause. She knew Danny could hear the evasion, the gaps in her story. "Don't do anything risky, Nora. We need you back in one piece."

"Copy that," she said, turning her gaze back towards the tangle of trees ahead. "I'll be cautious."

Silence lengthened across the radio waves, the connection fragile against the backdrop of towering granite and ancient pines.

She continued her ascent toward the whitebark groves, each step scraping against the raw nerves of discovery. The creature was shaping the mountain, removing the bears that had always held the high country in balance. And now three hikers had walked into that reshaped territory, following trails the bears no longer walked.

By midday she reached the first grove. The whitebark pines stood skeletal, their bark stripped in systematic vertical gouges. No bear did this. The ground beneath was churned, scattered with pine cones split open with surgical precision. And there—at the grove's edge—a configuration of stones arranged in a deliberate arc, each one placed with intent. Not natural. Not random. She crouched, tracing the arc with her fingers. The stones were arranged in a pattern she recognized: the same symbol from the journal, rendered in granite instead of ink. A threshold. A boundary drawn.

She photographed everything, her pulse quickening. This was a marker, territorial and deliberate. She was crossing a line the creature had drawn. And the tracks leading through it were fresh—the mud still wet, the edges of each print not yet softened by rain.

Her radio crackled again. "Nora, we've got a development. Found one of the hiker's packs near the south drainage. No body. Just the pack, laid out flat on a boulder. Deliberately placed."

Her stomach turned. "I'm close to that area. I'll check it out."

"Negative. We're sending a team. You need to pull back and rendezvous."

But Nora was already moving, following the drainage downslope. The creature was leaving breadcrumbs. She had seen it before—with the black bear, when the body had been arranged like an offering beneath the roots of a fallen cedar. It wanted her to follow. It wanted her to see.

She found the pack within an hour. Danny's description was accurate—laid flat, zippers open, contents arranged in a neat row beside it. Flashlight. Water bottle. Trail map. Granola bars. All untouched. All positioned with care. And tucked beneath the map, half-hidden, a single feather from an owl. It was still warm.

Nora's breath caught. She scanned the treeline. Nothing moved. But the warmth of the feather meant recent presence—not hours, but minutes.

And beside the arrangement, pressed into the soft mud: that same impossible footprint. Longer than a bear's, with claws that curved beyond the pads. Wrong gait. The print was fresh, still filling with rainwater. She pressed her finger to the edge. The mud gave way easily. Minutes old.

Nora crouched, her breath shallow. She was close. The tracks were getting fresher. The creature was no longer leaving evidence of where it had been. It was leaving evidence of where it was.

A sound reached her—low, resonant, vibrating up through the ground itself. The hum the old ranger had described in his journal. It seemed to come from everywhere and nowhere, a frequency that bypassed her ears and settled directly into her bones. This time, she could feel it in her teeth. Modulation. A rhythm beneath the drone, like a heartbeat slowed to geologic time.

She stood slowly, rifle ready, scanning the treeline. Nothing moved. But the hum continued, patient and deliberate, as if the mountain itself were breathing. And in the space between pulses, she heard something else: the soft scrape of a claw against stone. Somewhere ahead. Waiting.

Her radio crackled one more time. "Nora—" Danny's voice fractured with static. "—reading unusual seismic—" The signal dissolved into white noise.

She was alone.

The hum grew louder, and Nora turned toward its source, deeper into the whitebark country, where the answers waited in the heart of the boundary shift. The tracks ahead were no longer fading into the forest. They were clear, sharp, recent. The creature was not hiding anymore. It was leading her.

The journey through the dark had truly begun.
