Chapter 4

The Diary's Voice

The ranger station lay cold and silent when Nora finally pushed through the door, the forest still clinging to her in threads of pine and damp earth. She crossed directly to the cramped lab space she’d carved from a neglected utility closet, set the sample tube on the workbench, and clicked on the microscope’s lamp. The room snapped from shadow to sterile brightness.

She prepared the slide with methodical precision, her fingers steady even as the forest’s echoes thrummed beneath her skin. The thin sliver of skin beneath the lenses gave up its secrets reluctantly. She focused, adjusted, her breath shallow and even. Fine layers of the dermis resolved—smooth, unmarred—until she found what she sought: a patina of crystalline structures, pale amber catching the light, clustered tighter than nature should allow. Not cellular debris. Not blood. Tree resin. But from what tree?

Her mental catalog flicked through local flora. Not red cedar, not hemlock. The resin sparked recognition—a rare pine whose sap oozed viscous and gold, a reminder of ancient amber formations. Whitebark pine. The name surfaced with an uneasy pull. Those trees grew high, near timberline, in scattered groves that served as shelter for grizzlies after hibernation. Bears clawed the bark for the inner cambium. They cached cones. They knew the whitebark groves better than any ranger ever would.

She stared at the slide. The resin was not incidental. The tissue had come from a bear.

Nora wiped her hands on her thighs and crossed to her office, pulling the old leather-bound journal from a pile of papers. She’d borrowed it months ago from the retired ranger who’d left it behind when he moved to a nursing home in Ellensburg. At the time, she’d dismissed his cramped marginalia as the ramblings of a man who’d spent too many winters alone. Now she read with new eyes.

The journal covered three years of patrols in the high country around Holman Peak. On almost every page, the ranger had drawn small symbols beside his entries—a circle with a line through it, a triangle nested inside a larger triangle, a spiral that curled tight at its center. She’d assumed they were shorthand for weather conditions or trail hazards. But when she flipped back to the earliest entries, she found a legend, buried at the bottom of a page: *circle with line = sound heard. triangle = track found. spiral = bear carcass.*

She began tracing the symbols across the maps taped into the journal’s spine. The circles clustered along the western slopes. The triangles marked the drainage below Holman Peak. The spirals—each one a dead bear—were scattered in a crescent around the same area. Not random. Not territory disputes. The symbols drew a perimeter, as if something had herded the bears into a pocket and culled them systematically.

Nora leaned closer, her breath fogging the brittle paper. She found a page where the ranger had drawn a crude sketch: a bear’s paw print, and beside it, a larger print—longer, with claws that curved beyond the pads. Words scribbled beneath it: *Not a bear. Not a wolf. Wrong gait.*

She turned the page. The ranger had written, in block capitals: *WHITEBARK GROVES STRIPPED. THREE BEARS FOUND. MIGRATION PATHS SHIFTING SOUTH. CREATURE DOES NOT FOLLOW THEM. IT DRIVES THEM. IT USES THE TERRAIN.*

The word *drives* had been underlined three times.

Nora sat back, the weight of understanding settling into her chest. The resin on the tissue—whitebark—meant the bear had been in the high groves. The journal’s symbols showed the creature systematically clearing those groves, pushing bears into open ground, killing them not for food but to scatter their remains across the slopes like warning flags. The bear she’d sampled had been collateral, a piece of the creature’s larger design. The creature was not merely hunting. It was shaping the mountain, removing the bears that had always held the high country in balance, replacing their presence with its own.

Her radio broke the silence, a crackle of static followed by Danny’s clipped voice, urgent and thick with concern. Missing hikers. Three thru-hikers, last ping near Holman Peak. Every word tightened the coil in Nora’s chest.

She felt the tug of fear and responsibility, knowing the terrain intimately. The groves. The drainage. The perimeter the journal had traced. The hikers would have passed through whitebark country. They would have followed the trails the bears no longer walked.

“I’ll head in tonight,” Nora stated, resolve threading her voice.

Danny paused before consenting with a sigh. “Be careful out there, Nora.”

Minutes later, the office hummed with her preparation—the methodical gathering of supplies turned into a litany of survival: her rifle, checked and ready, extra magazines nested in her pack with quiet assurance; bear spray clipped within a swift reach. Essential tools arranged with practiced efficiency, each piece integral to her plan, her skin buzzing with the tangible hum of purpose.

Darkness had crept over the forest by the time she stepped outside, the irregular rhythm of rain beginning to patter against the station’s wooden siding. She pulled her hood tight and set off into the night, the treeline absorbing her presence with a predatory yawn. Thunder rolled across the mountain, a distant echo of what lay ahead, as Nora disappeared into the wild heart of the North Cascades, the darkness a mantle and a guide.
