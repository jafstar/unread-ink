# Council opinions round 2: charlock.app + non-.com TLD picks

## Claude

**charlock.app** is clever wordplay that unfortunately commits the cardinal sin of naming: it has a prior meaning that will dominate first impressions. Charlock is indeed a yellow-flowered weed, and while obscure, it's going to create friction. More problematically, on first read I parsed it as "Char-lock" (like a door lock made by someone named Char) before "character-lock" clicked. That split-second of confusion is a tax you'll pay every single time someone encounters the name.

The .app TLD actually works brilliantly here—it correctly signals "this is a tool you use" rather than "this is a gallery you browse" (.art) or "this is a stock library you license from" (.photo). For this product, .app > .art > .photo, no question.

**On TLD strategy for this product specifically:**
- **.app** = "professional tool" (best for your positioning)
- **.art** = "creative project/portfolio" (too precious, too gallery-like)
- **.studio** = "creative workspace" (solid second choice, broader than .app)
- **.ai** = "we do AI" (obvious but commodity, doesn't differentiate)
- **.io** = "tech startup" (fine but generic, crypto/web3 baggage)
- **.id** = literally perfect for identity-lock concept, but criminally underutilized TLD

Here's my actual answer: **I'd ship keep.id** from Round 1.

It's the only name that nails all three requirements: (1) pronounceable/spellable on first try, (2) names the actual differentiator (keeping identity consistent), (3) works as both verb and noun. "Keep the character locked" → "keep.id". The .id TLD does double duty—it's both "identity" and a TLD that makes linguistic sense.

charlock.app is *almost* there—the .app choice shows you're thinking correctly—but the name itself needs one more iteration. If you're married to explicit "lock" language, what about **idlock.app** or **lockid.app**? Clearer portmanteau, no weed confusion, same conceptual territory.

But honestly? **keep.id** is sitting right there, perfectly available, doing exactly what you need.

## Gemini

Alright, let's cut to the chase on **charlock.app**.

**My Honest Opinion on charlock.app:**

1.  **Does it land as "character lock"? No, not on first read.** The 'char-' prefix isn't strong enough to universally trigger "character" in this context. It lands as a made-up word, a possible typo, or, most damagingly, brings to mind "charlock" – the common weed. Associating a sophisticated AI tool with a persistent, unwanted plant is a significant branding misstep. It immediately loses any sense of control, innovation, or desirability.

2.  **How does .app compare to .art or .photo?** For a *tool*, **.app** is actually an excellent choice. It clearly signals "application," "software," or "utility," which is what this product is. It correctly differentiates it from an "art gallery" (.art) or a "stock library" (.photo). So, while "charlock" is a miss, the **.app** TLD is a strong signal for your product type.

3.  **Say it out loud:** "CHAR-lock.app" is easy enough to say and spell, but this ease is overshadowed by the meaning problem. "unstatic.art" is also easy to say and spell. The issue for "charlock" isn't pronunciation; it's immediate comprehension and the unfortunate existing association.

4.  **Is "character lock" too narrow?** This is a strategic question. While the product *can* do objects/scenes, your true differentiator is the *consistency* of an "IDENTITY-LOCK," and characters are its most powerful and compelling application. Naming after the strongest use case isn't inherently bad; it defines your niche. However, if you plan to aggressively expand into non-character consistency as a primary selling point soon, then "character lock" could feel limiting. The real problem here isn't the scope of "character lock," it's the *word* "charlock."

**Independent TLD Pick for this Product:**

Considering illustrators, indie creators, and the desire for consistent characters, if not .com, my top pick would be **.id**.
*   **.id** directly communicates "identity" and "identification," which is precisely what your "IDENTITY-LOCK" feature provides. It's potent, concise, and incredibly on-brand for establishing consistency.
*   **.studio** would be a close second, signaling a professional creative environment, but **.id** speaks more directly to your unique value proposition.
*   **.ai** is good for signaling technology but can become generic.
*   **.art** focuses on the output, not the tool.

**Which Name to Actually Ship?**

**Absolutely NOT charlock.app.** The weed association is a deal-breaker.

"unstatic.art" names the *mechanic* (static -> dynamic) but not the *differentiator* (the lock/consistency). It's too generic for what makes you special.

Between the Round 1 alternatives, **keep.id** stands out.
*   **keep.id**: It's short, memorable, and the ".id" TLD perfectly reinforces the core "IDENTITY-LOCK" differentiator. "Keep" subtly implies consistency, permanence, and retaining that identity. It's elegant and intelligent.

If I were to propose something new that's more explicit but still concise:
**IDLock.ai** or **IDLock.app**. This is direct, unambiguous, and leverages strong, relevant TLDs. "IDLock" is harder to misinterpret than "charlock."

**My Recommendation: Ship IDLock.ai or IDLock.app.**
It clearly states the unique value proposition ("ID Lock") and pairs it with a TLD that signals either the cutting-edge technology (.ai) or the practical software tool (.app). This is clear, differentiated, and avoids any unfortunate associations.

## Honest Opinion on `charlock.app`

**It reads as "charlock" before "character lock."** 

Here’s why: the word "charlock" is an established English word for a yellow-flowered weed (mustard family). It also sounds like a fantasy tavern name or a typo of "charlock" (like Sherlock’s less famous cousin). On first read, most people will see *char-lock* (rhymes with "car lock") and assume it’s a made-up brand name, not a compound of "character + lock." The semantic bridge is too fragile. You’re forcing your audience to do a double-take and consciously parse it—and in an attention economy, that’s a death sentence. One beat of confusion and they’re gone.

**.app is the wrong TLD for this product.** 

You are selling to illustrators, indie creators, and people who want consistent character art. These people live in .art, .studio, and .io. `.app` signals a utility SaaS tool (think calendar.app, todoist-like stuff). It says "cold, functional software." Your product is *creative* and *identity-centered*. `.app` actively undermines that. `.art` or `.studio` would be vastly more appropriate—they signal "this is a tool for your creative process, not a spreadsheet."

**Say it aloud:** "charlock dot app" — four syllables, hard consonant cluster, and you have to explain the "char" part every time. "unstatic" is two syllables, easy to spell, and the meaning is intuitive even if it misses the lock detail.

**On "character lock" being too narrow:** You’re right that the product now spans settings, scenes, and objects. But naming yourself after your shiniest feature is a classic startup mistake—until the next feature eclipses it. "Character lock" boxes you in. "Unstatic" at least captures the broader transformation.

**My pick:** I’d ship **`unstatic.studio`** (or `.art`, but `.studio` signals a workspace/toolkit vibe that fits illustrators better than `.art`, which feels gallery-ish). It’s phonetically clean, conceptually right, and leaves room to grow. Yes, it names the mechanic, not the lock. But it’s *gettable*. Name is about first-impression transmission, not feature documentation. Tell the lock story in your tagline. The name should *invite* the story, not *be* the story.

**Verdict:** Pass on charlock.app. Domain squatters will rejoice. Your users will squint.
